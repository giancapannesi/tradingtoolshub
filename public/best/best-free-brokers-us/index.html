Best Free US Brokers in 2026

Every major US broker now offers commission-free stock and ETF trading, but free does not mean identical. The real differences lie in margin rates, options fees, data quality, platform tools, and order execution. Fidelity provides the best overall free experience with price improvement that actually saves you money on execution. Charles Schwab gives you thinkorswim for free — the most powerful retail trading platform. Robinhood pioneered free trading but has limitations in research and order types. Webull offers impressive charting and extended trading hours at no cost. We break down what each broker actually costs beyond the zero-commission headline. We calculated the true annual cost of each broker based on typical trading patterns to show where zero commission becomes misleading.

Free US Brokers Are Not All Free in the Same Way

The best free U.S. broker is the one that keeps the whole workflow inexpensive, not just the one with a $0 stock commission headline. Fidelity is the strongest default for investors and swing traders because its published pricing combines $0 online U.S. stock, ETF and option trades with strong execution quality, research and no account minimum. Charles Schwab is the best free platform choice for traders who want thinkorswim without paying platform rent. Interactive Brokers Lite is the best free-stock choice for traders who may later need global markets, margin efficiency or a pro routing path. Webull and Robinhood are easiest for mobile-first traders, but the tradeoff is lighter research and a more app-led workflow.

Free should be read narrowly. It usually means no base commission for online U.S.-listed stocks and ETFs. Options contracts, index options, margin interest, regulatory fees, wire fees, ACAT transfers, data packages, futures, OTC stocks and broker-assisted trades can still matter.

What Free Does Not Include

The practical broker comparison starts after the headline commission line.

  • Options: Fidelity and Schwab publish $0 base commission with a $0.65 per-contract options fee. Robinhood has no per-contract fee on stock and ETF options, while index options have a contract fee plus exchange and regulatory charges.
  • Execution quality: a broker with a contract fee can still be cheaper than a no-fee broker if fills are consistently better. For active traders, a one-cent fill difference can matter more than a visible ticket fee.
  • Platform depth: thinkorswim, Active Trader Pro, IBKR Trader Workstation and Webull charts are different workflows. The best free broker is the one you can actually trade cleanly.
  • Margin: margin rates are not free. Compare published margin schedules before using leverage.
  • Market coverage: free stock trading does not mean free futures, forex, bonds, OTC stocks or international access.

If platform depth matters more than brokerage pricing, compare the best charting platforms. If the decision is between broker categories, start with the U.S. brokers hub and the full best U.S. brokers ranking.

Source Checks and Current Pricing Context

Fidelity publishes $0 commissions for online U.S. stock, ETF and option trades, with options contract fees still applying. Schwab publishes $0 online trades, no account or trade minimums and a $0.65 standard options contract fee. Interactive Brokers publishes IBKR Lite $0 U.S. stock and ETF commissions for U.S. residents, plus IBKR Pro pricing for active traders. Robinhood publishes no per-contract fees on stock and ETF options, while index options and other regulatory or exchange fees can still apply.

That makes the ranking more about fit than one universal winner. Fidelity is the safest free default. Schwab is the strongest free platform. IBKR is the upgrade path for serious traders. Webull and Robinhood are easiest to start, but the lighter workflows should be weighed against execution, research and account-depth requirements.

Bottom Line

Pick Fidelity if you want the strongest free all-rounder. Pick Schwab if free access to thinkorswim matters. Pick IBKR if you expect to outgrow a beginner brokerage. Use Webull or Robinhood only when the mobile-first workflow is more important than deep research, order routing and professional platform controls.
