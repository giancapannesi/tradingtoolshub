Journal Head-to-Head

Short answer: choose TradesViz if you want a modern web journal with broad asset support, dashboards, simulators and AI-style analysis features. Choose TradingDiary Pro if you want a Windows desktop journal, local data control, tax reports and predictable one-time pricing.

The Real Difference

TradesViz and TradingDiary Pro sit on opposite sides of the journal market. TradesViz is a web product built for traders who want dashboards, imports, reports, replay/simulator features and a lot of analysis in one place. TradingDiary Pro is older-school desktop software: local database, Windows workflow, one-time license, optional support renewal and deep reporting without a recurring SaaS subscription.

That makes the decision less about which one is "better" and more about how you want to work. If you expect to review trades from different machines, use newer analysis modules and keep upgrading as the product adds features, TradesViz is the natural fit. If you want the journal data on your own machine and dislike monthly subscriptions, TradingDiary Pro is still a credible choice.

Pricing and Ownership

TradesViz currently advertises a free Basic plan, paid Pro and Platinum plans, yearly savings, and a 7-day free trial for paid tiers. Its public pages also emphasize broader current features such as custom dashboards, simulators, replay, AI Q&A, options flow and backtesting. That makes the stored older profile understated; the product has clearly moved beyond a simple "100+ metrics" journal.

TradingDiary Pro's official pricing page lists a $149 one-time software fee and an optional $49 annual upgrade/support fee. That structure is attractive for traders who want to pay once, keep using the software and avoid another recurring subscription. The tradeoff is that desktop software puts more responsibility on the user for installation, backups and device access.

Where TradesViz Wins

TradesViz wins when the trader wants breadth and continuous product development. Its current public pages describe a product that includes journals, custom dashboards, AI Q&A, simulators, replay, backtesting and options-oriented modules. That is a stronger fit for active traders who want one analysis cockpit rather than a local reporting application.

The free plan also lowers friction. A trader can start journaling before deciding whether paid features are worth it. That matters because the biggest journaling failure is usually adoption, not missing one advanced metric.

Where TradingDiary Pro Wins

TradingDiary Pro wins for privacy-minded and subscription-averse traders. A local Windows journal is less convenient than a cloud product, but it gives the trader more direct control over the database. For traders who treat trade history as sensitive business data, that can matter more than a slicker interface.

The one-time price is also simple. If a trader uses the product for several years, the cost can be far lower than a paid monthly journal. The weak point is the same as the strength: desktop software is not ideal for traders who want browser access, mobile review or cloud sync.

Bottom Line

TradesViz is the better pick for most modern multi-asset traders who want a web journal with richer current features. TradingDiary Pro is the better pick for Windows traders who want local data, tax reporting and predictable long-term cost.
