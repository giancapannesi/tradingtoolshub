How to choose a US broker without getting distracted by the marketing

A US broker page should not rank brokers by a single headline commission number. Most serious traders already know that major US brokers advertise $0 online stock and ETF trades. The useful question is what happens after the first trade: options contract fees, margin rates, cash yield, short availability, fills, platform quality, research, tax reporting, retirement accounts, customer support, and whether the broker still feels good after you place hundreds of orders.

For long-term investors, start with Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and J.P. Morgan Self-Directed Investing. Fidelity is usually the strongest fit for research, retirement accounts, cash management, and a simple all-in-one brokerage account. Schwab is a very strong full-service choice, especially after the thinkorswim transition brought serious active-trader tooling into the Schwab ecosystem. J.P. Morgan makes the most sense for Chase customers who want simple investing next to banking rather than a specialist trading platform.

For active traders, the shortlist changes. Interactive Brokers is the most serious platform for global markets, margin efficiency, advanced order routing, and professional trading workflows. Tastytrade, E*TRADE, TradeStation, and Webull are more relevant when options, charting, mobile tools, or strategy workflows matter more than retirement-account convenience.

The wrong way to pick a broker is to ask which one is best in general. The right way is to map the broker to the job. A passive investor needs low friction, good account support, and research. A day trader needs speed, routing, platform stability, and margin clarity. An options trader needs chains, probability tools, assignment handling, contract fees, and fast adjustment workflows. A trader using several markets needs global access and consistent data.

Current source checks to keep in mind

Current official broker pricing pages confirm the broad pattern: Schwab and Fidelity advertise $0 online US stock and ETF trades, while online options trades usually still carry per-contract fees. Interactive Brokers separates its Lite and Pro pricing structures, with Lite aimed at commission-free US stock and ETF trading for US residents and Pro aimed at lower-cost professional routing and global market access. That means the headline commission table is only the beginning of the decision.

Before choosing a broker, read the current fee schedule directly on the broker site and check the details that match your trade type. Options, futures, OTC securities, foreign shares, broker-assisted trades, margin balances, data subscriptions, and account transfer fees can matter more than the advertised equity commission.
