Short answer: choose Chartlog if your review process is visual and you want a lower-cost journal built around chart screenshots and trade review. Choose Journalytix if you are a futures trader who needs real-time logging, session context and news-aware review.

The Real Difference

Chartlog and Journalytix are both trading journals, but they are not interchangeable. Chartlog is for traders who learn by looking back at the chart: entry, exit, setup, mistake, and the pattern that kept repeating. Journalytix is for traders who need the journal to follow the live futures session and capture what happened around the trade.

A visual day trader can get more practical value from Chartlog than from a bigger analytics product if screenshots make review faster. A futures trader trying to understand execution, news timing and rule discipline can outgrow a screenshot-first journal quickly. The best pick depends on what the trader needs to see during review.

Pricing and Scope

Chartlog's official pricing page currently shows paid plans starting at $14.99 per month, with higher tiers for more functionality. That makes it the easier entry point for traders who want a dedicated journal without paying specialist futures pricing.

Journalytix is more expensive in the TTH profile at $47 per month or $399 per year, but it is not trying to be a cheap generic journal. It is positioned around real-time futures journaling, risk context and session review. If that saves manual work or prevents funded-account mistakes, the higher price can be justified.

Where Chartlog Wins

Chartlog wins when fast visual review is the core habit. Many traders do not need another spreadsheet of stats; they need to see the chart and immediately recognize whether the setup was valid. A screenshot-first journal is especially useful for discretionary traders who make repeated pattern, timing or execution mistakes.

The price also helps. A lower monthly starting point makes Chartlog easier to test before the trader has proved that journaling is part of the weekly routine. That is important because unused journal subscriptions become another trading expense with no behavioral return.

It is also easier to explain inside a weekly review habit. A trader can open the trade, look at the chart, compare the planned setup with the actual execution and tag the mistake without turning the review into a data project. That makes Chartlog more useful for discretionary pattern traders than a heavy statistics product they never consistently review.

Where Journalytix Wins

Journalytix wins for futures traders who need the journal closer to execution. When the trade is tied to a session, a news release, a volatility shift or a funded-account rule, the review has to preserve more than a screenshot. Journalytix is better aligned with that job.

It is also the more serious option for traders who want the journal to enforce process. The higher price only makes sense if the trader is active enough to benefit from automation and context; casual traders should not pay for specialist futures workflow they will not use.

That distinction matters for prop-firm futures traders. A visual screenshot can show where the trade happened, but it may not tell the full story about session timing, risk exposure, or why the trader acted during a specific macro window. Journalytix is stronger when the review has to connect the trade to the trading day, not only to the chart.

Bottom Line

Chartlog is the practical choice for visual multi-asset traders who want a lower-cost journal. Journalytix is the better choice for futures traders who need real-time capture and session-aware review.
